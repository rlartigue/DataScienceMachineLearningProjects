1. Data Preparation and Overview¶

1.1 Business Case and Classification Task¶

Our dataset consists of FIFA soccer player data for 17588 players each with 53 corresponding features.

The classification task of our neural network is to use the FIFA dataset to classify individual soccer players into three classes of rank: Elite, Pro, and Rookie. The greater the class the better the soccer player.

Our classification algorithm has business application in the sports betting industry. Every year billions of dollars are won and lost in the sports betting industry, which is on track to becoming a $51 billion industry by 2019[1]. In past years a booming area in this industry has been in fantasy sports, in which a participants draft teams of players and bet of the outcome of their teams season.

Team performance is measured by the success of individual players on the team, which uses a point ranking system in which players are rewarded points for good plays, scoring goals, ect. Thus, the better the individual players on a team, the higher likelihood your team will outperform others. The difficulty of fantasy sports arises in choosing the best lineup with respect to an alloted budget/salary cap. Each player, based on their skills/ability cost varying amounts, with the best players costing the most.

Most participants rely on personal intuition when drafting players for his or her team. Using our classification algorithm, participants could select the highest skilled players with the lowest cost, allowing them to select more higher quality players while optimizing their budget.

Since predicting a players' skill classification is the cornerstone of our end application, we know that our accuracy needs to be quite high. This classification will be used to feed many other insights and recommendations along the way, and if our initial classifications are inaccurate, then our product/application will become increasingly inferior to our competitors. If we were attempting to be the first fantasy soccer prediction machine going to market, we could probably sacrifice some accuracy to deploy to the market faster, but since fantasy soccer is already a huge business, with many competitors offering up similar prediction models and fantasy lineup optimizers, we would need our accuracy to be very high in order to gain and retain customers who would subscribe to our product[2].

possible Extensions¶

For the sake of this lab, we arbitrarily picked our class buckets to evenly distribute the amount of players in each of our three classes (roughly 33% in each bucket). Realistically, if were to deploy this, we would explore the benefit of making the classes even more separable by adding more tiers. This would allow there to be a more distinguishable difference between the many levels of player skill that exist in the game.

Additionally, we could import fantasy player cost data into our dataset and make player recommendations during the draft. We could identify the best player available, based on the participants remaining budget and what positions the participant still needs for their lineup.

Lastly, we could analyze game data from the past to identify relationships between opponents. Do players/teams perform better against poor teams? Do players/teams perform below average against tough opponents? This would allow us to scale a players rating up or down based on their upcoming opponents, therefore making our lineup recommendations that much more powerful.

2. Evaluation¶

2.1. Evaluation Metric¶

In choosing an appropriate evaluation metric to evaluate our algorithms generalization performance, it is important to first understand the algorithms classification task and what we are trying to accomplish. The classification task of our algorithm, as stated in our business case, is to classify soccer players into three classes of corresponding skill level: Elite, Pro, and Rookie. Also, in our business case, we stated that our algorithm needs to be as accurate as possible in order to remain competitive with the many other deployed fantasy lineup drafting algorithms on the market today. Critical to choosing an evaluation metric to evaluate our algorithms generalization performance, we need to define exactly what accuracy means pertaining to our dataset. To further understand accuracy as it applies to our dataset, lets next look at the class distribution of players.

As can be seen from the distribution of classes in our dataset, there is an immense class imbalance between the classes. When we created our classes for our dataset (Elite (2), Pro (1), Rookie (0)), we created them based on loosely determined values that we felt effectively separated tiers of players in our dataset. In doing so, we unintentionally created a class imbalance in our tiers. The Elite class (2) having 2030 instances, the Pro class (1) having 11,866 instances, and the Rookie class (0) having 3692 instances.

Due to this class imbalance, when exploring different evaluation metrics, two primary metrics emerged as contenders: F1-Score and Log-Loss. While both of these seem like they will work, we leaned towards the macro F1-Score metric as it

Due to this class imbalance, we chose to use the macro averaged F1-Score as the evaluation metric for our algorithms performance. Using macro averaging allows for the precision in each class to be weighted equally across all classes. Thus a macro averaged F1 score computes the precision and recall metrics independently for each class, then takes the average, hence treating all classes equally. Thus even though class 1 has 11,866 instances and class 2 has 2030, the our algorithms classification performance for each of the classes gets the same weight in the macro averaged F1-Score. Therefore, the bias of the instance imbalance of the classes is removed from the evaluation of out dataset. To calculate the macro averaged F1-Score for our algorithm the harmonic mean is taken of the Macro averaged precision and recall shown below:

$$ Macro \hspace{2mm} Averaged \hspace{2mm} Precision = \frac{P\hspace{2mm}(Class\hspace{2mm}0) + P\hspace{2mm}(Class\hspace{2mm}1) + P\hspace{2mm}(Class\hspace{2mm}2)} 3$$$$ Macro \hspace{2mm} Averaged \hspace{2mm} Recall = \frac{R\hspace{2mm}(Class\hspace{2mm}0) + R\hspace{2mm}(Class\hspace{2mm}1) + R\hspace{2mm}(Class\hspace{2mm}2)} 3$$

In terms of the business case we argued for, we believe this is the appropriate evaluation metric to best capture the actual classification accuracy across our classes. To further understand why a macro averaged F1-Score is a more appropriate evaluation metric for our algorithm as opposed to lets say accuracy, lets take a step back.

Consider we chose accuracy as the evaluation metric for our algorithm. If we correctly classified all the instances in class 1 and class 0, and misclassified all the instances in class 2, our algorithm would have a classification accuracy score of:

$$ \frac{11866 + 3692} {11866 + 3692 + 2030} = 0.88 $$

or 88 %. Wow! 88% percent accuracy!

While 88 % accuracy gives the perception that our algorithm is very accurately classifying players, it is in fact not because it was not able to correctly classify any class 2 instances. For this reason, accuracy can be very misleading when working with datasets with large class imbalances as seen in ours.

Thus, in terms of the business case, our algorithm would need to accurately classify players across ALL classes equally well to be an effective and deplorable fantasy lineup drafting algorithm. Which is why the appropriate evaluation metric for our algorithms generalization performance is the macro averages F1-Score.

2.2. Method for Generating Training and Testing Sets¶

Again we choosing an appropriate method for generating training and testing sets from our dataset, it is crucial to keep in mind the immense class imbalance in our dataset. Due to this class imbalance, the appropriate method for splitting our data into training and testing sets in Stratified K-Fold Cross-Validation.

Similar to the K-Fold cross validation technique, where the data is split into K number of folds, where K-1 folds are used for training and 1 fold is used for testing, stratified K-Fold cross validation performs the same splitting of the data. However, in stratified K-Fold techniques, each fold is representative of the original dataset. Thus, if the dataset is 90 % class A and 10 % class B, Stratified K-Fold Cross-Validation ensures that each fold will also have 90 % class A instances and 10 % class B instances, mirroring the original distribution of the dataset. This is greatly important when dealing with class imbalances in datasets, because it ensures no folds are 100 % class A and 0 % class B, which makes for a poor representation of the original dataset, and therefore a poor classifier.

class CustomNLayerPerceptron(object):
    def __init__(self, n_neurons = 50, n_hidden_layers = 1, C = 0.1, epochs = 10, eta = 0.01, random_state = 1, phi = 'sigmoid', cost = 'quad'):
        np.random.seed(random_state)
        self.n_neurons = n_neurons
        self.n_hidden_layers = n_hidden_layers
        self.C = C
        self.epochs = epochs
        self.eta = eta
        self.phi = phi
        self.cost = cost 
     
    @staticmethod
    def _encode_labels(y):
        
        onehot = pd.get_dummies(y).values.T
        return onehot
    
    @staticmethod
    def _initialize_weights(self):
        W = [] 
        # Initial Layer
        W1_num_elems = (self.n_features_ + 1) * self.n_neurons
        W1 = np.random.uniform(-1.0, 1.0, size = W1_num_elems)
        W1 = W1.reshape(self.n_neurons, self.n_features_ + 1)
        W.append(W1)
        
        # Hidden Layers
        for i in range(self.n_hidden_layers - 1):
            W_hidden_num_elems = ((self.n_neurons + 1) * (self.n_neurons))
            W_hidden = np.random.uniform(-1.0, 1.0, size = W_hidden_num_elems)
            W_hidden = W_hidden.reshape(self.n_neurons, self.n_neurons + 1) # Number of hiddden neurons + bias x number of hidden neurons + bias
            W.append(W_hidden)
        
        # Final Layer
        W2_num_elems = (self.n_neurons + 1) * self.n_output_
        W2 = np.random.uniform(-1.0, 1.0, size = W2_num_elems)
        W2 = W2.reshape(self.n_output_, self.n_neurons + 1)
        W.append(W2)
        
        self.W_ = W
        
        return W


    # Activation Function Selection
    def _activation(self, z):
        if self.phi == 'sigmoid':
            return self._phi_sigmoid(z)
        elif self.phi == 'linear':
            return self._phi_linear(z)
        elif self.phi == 'ReLU':
            return self._phi_ReLU(z)
        elif self.phi == 'Swish':
            return self._phi_Swish(z)
        
    @staticmethod
    def _phi_sigmoid(z):
        return expit(z)
    
    @staticmethod # without this here I got an error saying _phi_linear() takes 1 positional argument but 2 were given... interesting??
    def _phi_linear(z):
        return z
    
    @staticmethod
    def _phi_ReLU(z):
        return np.maximum(0,z.copy()) # this way is better
    
    @staticmethod
    def _phi_Swish(z):
        return z*expit(z)
    
    # Cost Function Selection
    def _cost(self, A, Y_enc, W):
        if self.cost == 'quad':
            return self._cost_quadratic(A, Y_enc, W)
        elif self.cost == 'crossentropy':
            return self._cost_cross_entropy(A, Y_enc, W)
    
    def _cost_quadratic(self, A, Y_enc, W):
        cost = np.mean((Y_enc - A) ** 2)
        L2_term = self._L2_reg(self.C, W)
        return cost + L2_term
    
    def _cost_cross_entropy(self, A3, Y_enc, W):
        cost = -np.mean(np.nan_to_num((Y_enc*np.log(A3)+(1-Y_enc)*np.log(1-A3))))
        L2_term = self._L2_reg(self.C, W)
        return cost + L2_term
    
    def _L2_reg(self, C, W):
        mean = 0
        for w in self.W:
            mean += np.mean(w[:, 1:] ** 2)
        return(C/len(self.W) * np.sqrt(mean))
    
    @staticmethod
    def _add_bias_unit(X, how = 'column'):
        if how == 'column':
            ones = np.ones((X.shape[0], 1))
            X_new = np.hstack((ones,X)) # horizontal stack
        elif how == 'row':
            ones = np.ones((1, X.shape[1]))
            X_new = np.vstack((ones, X)) # vertical stack    
        return X_new
    
    def _feedforward(self, X, W):
        A = []
        Z = []
        
        A1 = self._add_bias_unit(X.T, how = 'row')
        A.append(A1)
        Z1 = W[0] @ A[0]
        Z.append(Z1)
        
        for idx in range(self.n_hidden_layers):
            Ah = self._activation(Z[idx])
            Ah = self._add_bias_unit(Ah, how = 'row')
            A.append(Ah)
            Zh = W[idx+1] @ A[idx + 1]
            Z.append(Zh)
        
        A.append(self._activation(Z[-1]))
        
        self.A_ = A
        self.Z_ = Z
        
        return A, Z

    def _get_gradient(self, A, Z, W, Y_enc):
        
        V = self.W.copy()
        G = self.W.copy()
        
        V[-1] = self._get_cost_derivative(A[-1], Y_enc) * self._activation_derivative(A[-1])
        G[-1] = V[-1] @ A[-2].T
        G[-1] += W[-1] * self.C
        
        
        
        for i in range(-2, -self.n_hidden_layers-2, -1):
            if i == -2:
                V[i] = (self._activation_derivative(A[i])) * (W[i+1].T @ V[i+1])
            else:
                V[i] = (self._activation_derivative(A[i])) * (W[i+1].T @ V[i+1][1:,:])
            
            G[i] = V[i][1:, :] @ A[i-1].T
            G[i][:, 1:] += W[i][:, 1:] * self.C
            
        self.G_ = G
        
        return G
    
    def _get_cost_derivative(self, A, Y_enc):
        if self.cost == 'quad':
            cost_deriv = -2 * (Y_enc - A)
            return cost_deriv
        elif self.cost == 'crossentropy':
            cost_deriv = A - Y_enc
            return cost_deriv
            
    def _activation_derivative(self, A):
        if self.phi == 'sigmoid':
            act_deriv = A * (1 - A)
            return act_deriv
        elif self.phi == 'linear':
            act_deriv = A * 1.0
            return act_deriv
        elif self.phi == 'ReLU':
            act_deriv = (A > 0) * 1.0
            return act_deriv
        elif self.phi == 'Swish':
            act_deriv = ((A * expit(A)) + expit(A) * (1 - (A * expit(A))))
            return act_deriv
    
    def predict(self, X):
        _, A = self._feedforward(X, self.W)
        y_pred = np.argmax(A[-1], axis = 0)
        return y_pred
    
    def fit(self, X, y):
        X_data, y_data = X.copy(), y.copy()
        Y_enc = self._encode_labels(y)
        self.n_features_ = X_data.shape[1]
        self.n_output_ = Y_enc.shape[0]
        self.W = self._initialize_weights(self)
        
        self.cost_ = []
        self.G1_avg_ = []
        self.G2_avg_ = []
        self.G3_avg_ = []
        self.G4_avg_ = []
        
        for i in range(self.epochs):
            A, Z = self._feedforward(X_data, self.W)
            cost = self._cost(A[-1], Y_enc, self.W)
            self.cost_.append(cost)
            self.G = self._get_gradient(A = A, Z = Z, Y_enc = Y_enc, W = self.W)
            # This is for plotting the gradients with out optimal parameters
            if (len(self.G)) == 1:
                self.G1_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[0])))
            elif (len(self.G)) == 2:
                self.G1_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[0])))
                self.G2_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[1])))
            elif (len(self.G)) == 3:
                self.G1_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[0])))
                self.G2_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[1])))
                self.G3_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[2])))
            elif (len(self.G)) == 4:
                self.G1_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[0])))
                self.G2_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[1])))
                self.G3_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[2])))
                self.G4_avg_.append(abs(np.mean(self.G[3])))
            else: 
                pass
            
            for idx in range(self.n_hidden_layers+1):
                self.W[idx] -= self.eta * self.G[idx]
        return self
    
    def get_params(self, deep = True):
        return {'n_neurons': self.n_neurons,
                'n_hidden_layers': self.n_hidden_layers,
                'C': self.C,
                'epochs': self.epochs,
                'eta': self.eta,
                'phi': self.phi,
                'cost': self.cost}
    
    def set_params(self, **params):
        for param, value in params.items():
            self.__setattr__(param, value)
            
        return self
